Why Do I Always Make the Wrong Decision?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

You make a choice. It goes wrong.

You make another. Same.

And now you've started to believe that something about your judgment is broken.

The short answer The belief that you always choose wrong is a story your mind builds from selected evidence. It overlooks the times you chose well, and it mistakes outcomes — which are never fully in your control — for decisions, which are.
What would it mean about you if your judgment was actually fine but the situations were genuinely hard?

Frequently asked

Why do I feel like I always make wrong decisions?

Usually because you're evaluating decisions by their outcomes rather than the quality of the reasoning at the time. A decision made with good information and values can still turn out badly — and a lucky one can turn out well. Conflating outcome with decision quality leads to a distorted self-assessment.

How can I make better decisions?

Start by separating what you knew when you decided from what you know now. Then assess the process: did you have relevant information? Did you consider what mattered to you? Did you have enough time? Improving decision-making is about improving the process — not guaranteeing the outcome.

Is it possible to trust yourself again after making a lot of 'bad' choices?

Yes — but it requires examining what you actually mean by 'bad choices.' Often what looks like a pattern of poor judgment is really a pattern of hard circumstances, or choices made under pressure or with limited options. Reclaiming trust in yourself starts with giving yourself accurate credit.
