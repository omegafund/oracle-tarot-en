Why Do I Avoid Decisions That Could Change My Life?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

You know the decision is there. You've known for a while.

But somehow there's always a reason it's not quite the right time.

And the longer it sits, the heavier it seems to get.

The short answer Avoiding a life-altering decision often isn't about the decision itself — it's about not wanting to be responsible for the outcome, good or bad. As long as the choice stays unmade, you can hold onto every possibility at once.
Ask yourself: what would you have to give up believing about yourself, or your options, the moment you actually decided?

  • whether you're avoiding the decision or avoiding responsibility for it

Signs this may be true for you

  • You've thought about this decision for a long time without acting.
  • You wait for outside circumstances to force your hand.
  • You feel a mix of relief and dread when you imagine finally choosing.
  • You keep researching or talking about it instead of deciding.

Frequently asked

Why do life-changing decisions feel harder to make than small ones?

Because they carry more perceived weight and permanence, and often touch on identity, not just logistics — making the wrong choice can feel like it says something bigger about who you are.

Is avoiding a decision itself a kind of decision?

In practice, yes — not deciding usually means the status quo continues, which is its own outcome, just one arrived at passively rather than actively.

How can I make a big decision feel less overwhelming?

Breaking it into smaller, reversible steps rather than treating it as one irreversible leap can make the process feel more manageable and less paralyzing.
