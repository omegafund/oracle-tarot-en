How Do I Know If It’s the Right Choice?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

You want to be sure. You want a sign, a feeling, a guarantee that you won’t regret it.

But the certainty you’re waiting for may not exist — and waiting for it might be the real risk.

This reflection looks at what ‘the right choice’ actually means, and why certainty rarely comes first.

The short answer The ‘right choice’ is rarely revealed by certainty beforehand — most meaningful decisions feel uncertain precisely because they matter and the outcome is unknown. Waiting to feel sure often just delays a choice you’ll have to make anyway with the same incomplete information. A more workable question is which option aligns with your values and which fear you’d rather not let decide for you.
Ask yourself: are you waiting for certainty, or for permission — and who would you need it from?

Frequently asked

How do I know I'm making the right decision?

Most consequential decisions don’t come with advance certainty — if they did, they wouldn’t feel like decisions. Rather than waiting to feel sure, it tends to help to ask which option aligns with your values, which one you’d choose if fear weren’t driving, and which regret you could live with. ‘Right’ is usually built through commitment after the choice, not discovered before it.

Why do I need to be certain before deciding?

The need for certainty is often the need to avoid regret or being wrong. But certainty is a feeling, not a fact, and waiting for it can become a way to never decide — which is itself a choice with its own consequences. Accepting that some uncertainty is unavoidable is often what finally frees the decision.

What if I make the wrong choice?

Most choices are more recoverable than they feel in the moment, and many ‘wrong’ choices become right through what you do afterward. Fear of the wrong choice tends to assume decisions are permanent and outcomes are knowable — usually neither is fully true. Choosing and adjusting beats waiting indefinitely for a guarantee that won’t arrive.
