Why Do I Keep Going Back to Someone I Know Is Wrong for Me?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

You've ended it. Maybe more than once.

And then — for a reason that's hard to explain afterward — you go back.

Not because you've forgotten what went wrong. But because something is stronger than what you know.

The short answer The pull back to someone harmful is rarely about the person and almost always about what they provide — or what the relationship has come to mean as a source of familiarity, intensity, or unfinished emotional business.

Signs this may be true for you

  • You always have a reason that makes sense in the moment but less sense afterward.
  • The relationship feels different every time you return — then becomes the same.
  • You keep a channel open even when you say you're done.
  • The pull is strongest when you're lonely, stressed, or anxious.

Frequently asked

Why do I keep returning to relationships I know are bad for me?

Because familiarity can feel like safety even when it isn't safe. The nervous system prefers the known, even when the known is painful, over the unknown. There's also often a fantasy at play — the belief that this time will be different — that's hard to fully abandon until it's finally, completely tested.

Is this a trauma bond?

Trauma bonding happens when cycles of harm and repair create an intense, hard-to-break attachment. It's more common than people realize and doesn't require dramatic abuse — it can form through intermittent warmth and coldness, hope and disappointment. If the pull back feels compulsive rather than chosen, that's worth exploring with a professional.

How do I actually stay away?

Not through willpower alone — that rarely holds. What actually helps is reducing contact to the point where you can't impulsively act on the pull, building a life where the emptiness that drives you back gets filled differently, and sitting with the grief of the ending rather than using the return to avoid it.
