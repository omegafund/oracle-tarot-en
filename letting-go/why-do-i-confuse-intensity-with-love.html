Why Do I Confuse Intensity With Love?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

The highs were dizzying. The lows were devastating. It felt like the realest thing you’d ever known.

But looking back, you wonder whether what gripped you was love — or just how much it made you feel.

This reflection looks at why intensity can be mistaken for connection, and what real closeness feels like instead.

The short answer Intensity is easy to mistake for love because it floods you with feeling — but feeling a lot is not the same as being well-loved. Drama, uncertainty, and emotional extremes create a powerful charge that the body reads as significance, especially if early experiences taught you that love comes with anxiety. Real closeness tends to feel steadier and safer, which can read as “less” only because it lacks the familiar adrenaline.
Ask yourself: did the relationship feel meaningful because it was nourishing, or because it kept you on edge?

Frequently asked

Why do I find calm relationships boring?

If your nervous system learned to associate love with intensity — unpredictability, longing, the chase — then calm can register as absence rather than safety. The lack of adrenaline gets misread as a lack of feeling. It doesn’t mean you’re incapable of steady love; it means steadiness is unfamiliar, and unfamiliar often feels wrong before it feels good.

Is intense attraction a sign of real love?

Not necessarily. Intense attraction can signal genuine connection, but it can just as easily signal anxiety, novelty, or an old pattern being activated. Intensity tells you something is being stirred up — it doesn’t tell you what. The clearer signal of love is how you feel over time and in the quiet stretches, not how high the early spark burns.

How do I tell the difference between passion and anxiety?

They can feel almost identical in the body — racing heart, preoccupation, urgency. The difference shows in the aftermath: passion tends to leave you feeling expanded and connected, while anxiety leaves you depleted and craving reassurance. Noticing how you feel between the peaks, rather than during them, is usually the most honest tell.
