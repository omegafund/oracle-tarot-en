Why Can't I Forgive Someone Who Hurt Me?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

People tell you to forgive. For your own sake.

But the hurt is still there, and forgiveness feels like saying it was okay.

It doesn't feel okay.

The short answer Forgiveness doesn't mean what happened was acceptable. It's about releasing yourself from being organized around the hurt — not for their benefit, but so the wound stops running your present.
What would forgiving them actually require you to give up — and is any part of the anger keeping you connected to them?

Signs this may be true for you

  • Thinking about forgiving them feels like betraying yourself or minimizing what happened.
  • You rehearse the hurt, the story, the evidence of what they did.
  • You want to be free of their effect on you, but can't imagine forgiving them.

Frequently asked

Does forgiving someone mean what they did was okay?

No — and this misunderstanding is what makes forgiveness feel impossible. Forgiveness is a decision to stop letting the wound run your present — which you do for yourself, not for them.

What if they don't deserve to be forgiven?

Forgiveness isn't about desert — it's about your freedom.

How do I start the process of forgiving?

Usually not through a single decision but through accumulated small shifts: grieving the actual loss and finding moments where the grip of the hurt relaxes slightly.
