Why Can’t I Let Go of the Version of Them I Imagined?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

Maybe the hardest part isn’t losing them. It’s losing who you thought they were.

You fell for a version — the potential, the promise, the person they were on their best days.

This reflection looks at the gap between who they were and who you needed them to be.

The short answer What’s often hardest to release isn’t the real person but the imagined one — the potential you saw, the future you built, the best-day version you treated as the truth. The grief is real because that imagined person felt real to you. Letting go means mourning a version that never fully existed, which is its own distinct kind of loss.
Ask yourself: which version are you grieving — who they actually showed you they were, or who you believed they could become?

Frequently asked

Why do I love someone's potential instead of who they are?

Falling for potential is often about the story we attach to it — the future we imagine, the version of ourselves we’d be with them at their best. It can also be a way to stay hopeful in a situation that isn’t working: focusing on who they could become makes it easier to overlook who they’re actually being. The grief comes when reality finally outweighs the imagined version.

How do I let go of who I thought they were?

Letting go of the imagined version means grieving it as a real loss — because to you, it was real. That involves honestly separating what they showed you from what you hoped for, and allowing yourself to mourn the future you’d built around the hope. It’s slower than getting over the person, because you’re releasing a story, not just a relationship.

Is it normal to grieve a version of someone that didn't exist?

Completely. In fact it’s often the deeper grief, because the imagined version carried all your hope and none of the disappointment. Mourning it isn’t foolish — it’s recognizing that the loss includes a future you genuinely believed in. Naming that you’re grieving the imagined person, not the real one, is what allows the real letting-go to begin.
