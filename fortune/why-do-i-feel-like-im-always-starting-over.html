Why Do I Feel Like I'm Always Starting Over?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

New job, new city, new routine — again.

It's not that you can't start things. You're good at that part.

It's that you rarely seem to get to keep building on what came before.

The short answer Feeling like you're always starting over can reflect an actual pattern of disruption, but it can also come from an internal habit of abandoning things before they mature — sometimes out of fear that stability itself won't last.
Ask yourself: when you look back, are the fresh starts something that happened to you, or something you kept choosing?

Signs this may be true for you

  • You feel a pull to leave once something becomes familiar or routine.
  • You have more beginnings than completions across various life areas.
  • You associate staying too long with being stuck.
  • You feel more comfortable with the excitement of new than the depth of established.

Frequently asked

Is it bad to start over often?

Not inherently — some people genuinely thrive through reinvention. It becomes worth examining when the pattern feels compulsive or leaves you without anything that's had time to deepen.

Why do I feel restless once things become stable?

Stability can sometimes trigger discomfort if it wasn't reliably available earlier in life, making change feel more familiar — and paradoxically safer — than staying put.

How can I tell if I should start over again or stay?

It helps to distinguish between leaving toward something meaningful and leaving simply to escape discomfort with routine — the first tends to feel purposeful, the second tends to feel automatic.
