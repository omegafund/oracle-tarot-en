Why Do I Keep Testing People I Love?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

You pull back a little, just to see if they notice.

You create small obstacles, half-consciously, and watch how they respond.

It's not a game you meant to play — but you keep playing it anyway.

The short answer Testing people you love is usually an attempt to get proof of commitment before fully trusting it — often because trust was previously broken, or because waiting to be let down feels safer than hoping without evidence.
Ask yourself: what result from the test would actually be enough to make you stop testing?

Signs this may be true for you

  • You create situations to see how someone will react.
  • You withdraw affection to gauge their response.
  • You feel a specific relief when someone "passes" a test you didn't announce.
  • You've been told your behavior feels like a setup, even when it isn't intended that way.

Frequently asked

Why do I test people instead of just trusting them?

Testing often develops as a protective strategy after past betrayal or inconsistency — it can feel safer to gather proof indirectly than to risk trusting and being wrong.

Does testing people ever actually build trust?

Rarely in a lasting way — because the doubt tends to resurface regardless of the outcome, requiring another test. Trust built through direct communication and consistent experience tends to hold better.

How can I stop testing people I care about?

Naming the underlying fear directly — to yourself, and sometimes to the other person — tends to be more effective than indirect testing, which can create the very distance you're trying to avoid.
