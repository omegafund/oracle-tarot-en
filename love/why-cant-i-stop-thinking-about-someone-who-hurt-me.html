Why Can’t I Stop Thinking About Someone Who Hurt Me?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

You know how it ended. You know how it felt. You know, logically, that it’s over.

And still — they live in your head, rent-free, at the worst hours.

This reflection looks at why the mind holds on to what hurt it, and what holding on is really doing for you.

The short answer Struggling to let go of someone who hurt you usually isn’t about them — it’s about an ending that was never fully grieved or made sense of. The mind loops on unfinished business: the closure you didn’t get, the version of them you hoped for, the meaning you’re still trying to extract. The attachment persists because part of you is still trying to resolve something that only you can close from the inside.
Ask yourself: what are you still hoping to get from this — an apology, an explanation, a different ending — that you may need to give yourself instead?

Frequently asked

Why do I miss someone who treated me badly?

Missing someone who hurt you often isn’t about the harm — it’s about the good moments and the hope attached to them, which the mind preserves more vividly than the pain. There’s also the unfinished quality: when an ending feels unresolved, the mind keeps returning to it, trying to complete a story that was left open. Missing them is rarely a sign you should go back; more often it’s grief looking for closure.

How do I stop thinking about my ex?

Trying to force the thoughts away tends to amplify them. What works better is addressing what the thoughts are doing — usually seeking closure, meaning, or relief from grief. Giving yourself the acknowledgment you were waiting for them to provide, and letting yourself actually feel the loss rather than loop on it, gradually loosens the grip. The thoughts fade as the unfinished feeling gets finished internally.

Is it normal to still have feelings for someone who hurt me?

Entirely normal. Feelings don’t obey logic or timelines, and attachment doesn’t switch off because someone proved unworthy of it. Having lingering feelings doesn’t mean you should act on them or that the relationship was right — it means you’re human and still processing. The feelings and the decision to stay away can coexist.
