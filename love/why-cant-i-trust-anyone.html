Why Can't I Trust Anyone?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

You want to let people in. Part of you does.

But there's a wall that goes up automatically — before you've even decided to build it.

And you're not sure if you're protecting yourself or punishing yourself.

The short answer Difficulty trusting is almost always a scar, not a personality trait. Something — or a series of things — taught your nervous system that people aren't safe to depend on. That lesson made sense when it formed. It may no longer be accurate.
Ask yourself: when was the first time you learned that trusting someone could hurt you?

Signs this may be true for you

  • You assume people's motives are worse than they appear.
  • You withdraw when people get too close, even if things are going well.
  • You test people — sometimes without realizing it.
  • You feel more relieved than happy when someone proves trustworthy.

Frequently asked

Why can't I trust people even when they haven't hurt me?

Because trust isn't fully conscious — it's a felt sense managed by the nervous system. If the nervous system learned that closeness is dangerous, it will sound the alarm even in safe situations. The mind might recognize someone is trustworthy while the body stays braced for the hurt.

Can you learn to trust again after being betrayed?

Yes — though it's usually a slower process than the original trust was. It tends to happen through repeated small experiences of reliability rather than one big moment of decision. And it requires some degree of risk — you can't build trust without exposing yourself to the possibility of being wrong.

Is it possible to be too careful with trust?

Hypervigilance has a cost. When you're constantly screening for threat, you're also filtering out genuine connection. The energy spent on protective distance is energy not available for intimacy. At some point, the protection that once kept you safe starts limiting what's possible.
