Why Do They Keep Pulling Away?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

Things feel close — then suddenly they’re distant again.

You replay what you said, what you did, looking for the moment it shifted. But the pattern keeps repeating, no matter how careful you are.

This reflection looks at what the distance might be protecting — in them, and in you.

The short answer When someone repeatedly pulls away, the withdrawal is usually less about you and more about their own discomfort with closeness — a learned move that creates safety when intimacy feels like too much. The harder question is what their distance activates in you, because the cycle is held in place by both sides. Seeing your own part in the loop is what gives you something you can actually change.
Before reading on: when they pull away, what do you do — and is that response calming the situation or quietly feeding it?

Frequently asked

Why does someone pull away when things are going well?

Counterintuitively, growing closeness is exactly what triggers withdrawal in people who associate intimacy with risk. When connection deepens past their comfort zone, distance becomes a way to regulate the anxiety it stirs up. It often has little to do with how things are objectively going — the very fact that things are going well is what makes it feel unsafe to them.

Should I pull away too or give them space?

Matching their withdrawal as a tactic usually backfires, because it turns a connection into a contest. Genuine space — given from a settled place rather than as punishment or strategy — is different, and often healthier. The clearer move is to notice your own urgency first: if you can self-regulate instead of chasing or retaliating, you stop feeding the cycle and see more accurately what’s actually happening.

How do I know if they're losing interest or just scared?

Fear and fading interest can look identical from the outside — both create distance. The difference shows over time and in the return: someone scared of closeness tends to come back, then retreat, in a rhythm; someone genuinely disengaging drifts steadily without the pull back. Rather than decoding every signal, watching the overall pattern across weeks tells you far more than any single moment.
