Why Do I Miss Someone Who Hurt Me?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

You know they weren't good for you. You know.

And still — there it is. The missing.

Not for what they were, necessarily. But for something.

The short answer Missing someone who hurt you doesn't mean you've forgotten the hurt, or that you should go back. It means you're grieving something real — a version of them, a version of yourself, a possibility that existed before the pain did.
What specifically do you miss — the actual person, or the feeling you had at the beginning?

Signs this may be true for you

  • You find yourself thinking about good memories even when you know the relationship was harmful.
  • You feel guilty for missing them, which makes the missing worse.
  • You understand logically they weren't right for you, but that doesn't stop the feeling.
  • Some days the missing feels like proof you should try again.

Frequently asked

Is it normal to miss someone who hurt me?

Completely normal. Attachment and pain aren't opposites — they often coexist in the same relationship. You can be genuinely hurt by someone and still miss them, because what you formed with them was also real. The missing isn't evidence that you should have stayed or that what happened was okay.

Why does my brain keep going back to the good times?

Memory is selective and self-protective. The brain tends to replay positive experiences more readily than painful ones — partly as comfort, partly as unfinished business. The good memories feel urgent because they represent something you wanted and didn't fully get to keep.

How do I stop missing someone who wasn't good for me?

You probably can't stop the feeling on demand — but you can change your relationship to it. When the missing comes, let it be information: 'I miss feeling wanted' or 'I miss the version of me I was then.' That's more useful than 'I miss them,' because it points to what you actually need going forward.
