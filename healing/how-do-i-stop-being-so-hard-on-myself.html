How Do I Stop Being So Hard on Myself?

Created as a space for personal reflection — not therapy or diagnosis.

You make a mistake, and the voice starts.

You replay it. You name what it says about you. You extend the punishment past what any reasonable sentence would be.

And somewhere you know this isn't helping — but you don't know how to stop.

The short answer Self-criticism often masquerades as high standards or accountability. But there's a meaningful difference between honest self-assessment and punishing self-talk. The inner critic is rarely accurate — it's usually a voice learned from somewhere else, now running on automatic.
If you said to a friend what you say to yourself after making a mistake, how would they feel — and would you think that was fair?

Frequently asked

Why am I so hard on myself?

The inner critic is almost always an internalized voice — a parent, teacher, or environment that communicated that you needed to be different or better in order to be acceptable. That voice became part of your self-regulation system. It may have helped you once. It's probably not helping you now.

Does being hard on yourself actually make you perform better?

Research suggests the opposite — chronic self-criticism tends to undermine performance, creativity, and motivation. It activates the threat system, which narrows focus and reduces the willingness to try things where you might fail. Self-compassion, by contrast, tends to support honest accountability without the shutdown.

How do I develop self-compassion?

Start by noticing — really noticing — what you say to yourself after a mistake. Then ask: would I say this to someone I love? If not, what would I say instead? The practice is simply that: catching the voice, asking the question, and substituting the response you'd give someone you actually care about.
